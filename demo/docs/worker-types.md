# Worker Types and Statutory Obligations — Nigeria

Nigeria recognises five main categories of workers, each with different employment terms and statutory obligations. Getting the classification right matters because it determines which taxes and contributions apply.

## The five worker types

### Full-time
A full-time worker is on a permanent, indefinite contract. They work standard hours and receive the full set of statutory benefits: PAYE, pension, NSITF, and NHF if opted in. Full-time is the default employment type for permanent staff.

### Part-time
A part-time worker works regular but reduced hours on an indefinite contract. They receive the same full statutory benefits as full-time workers — PAYE, pension, NSITF, and NHF if opted in. The only difference from full-time is the number of hours worked.

### Fixed-term
A fixed-term worker has a defined start date and end date. The employment ends automatically when the contract expires unless renewed. Fixed-term workers receive full statutory benefits — PAYE, pension, NSITF, and NHF if opted in — for the duration of the contract. Contract expiry should be tracked and workers notified before the end date.

### Casual
A casual worker is engaged on an irregular, as-needed basis with no guaranteed hours. Statutory treatment depends on the length of engagement:

- If a casual worker is engaged for **fewer than 30 days**, they are exempt from pension contributions. PAYE and NSITF still apply if they are on the payroll.
- If a casual worker is engaged for **30 or more days** — even if the engagement was originally intended to be short — pension contributions become mandatory: 8% employee and 10% employer of monthly emoluments.

Mark a casual worker as pension-exempt only if they are genuinely engaged for under 30 days. If the engagement extends beyond 30 days, pension must be calculated and remitted from that point.

### Contractor
A contractor (independent service provider) is not an employee and is handled entirely differently:

- Contractors are **not on the payroll** and are not included in the monthly payroll run.
- No PAYE is deducted from contractor payments.
- No pension contributions apply to contractors.
- Contractors are **not included** in the NSITF payroll calculation.
- The paying company deducts **5% WHT (Withholding Tax)** from every contractor payment before remitting the net amount to the contractor.
- WHT deducted must be remitted to FIRS by the **21st of the following month** via TaxPro Max.
- A WHT credit note must be issued to the contractor after each remittance.

| PAYE | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | No |

## Misclassification risk
If a contractor works exclusively for one business over a long period and is indistinguishable from an employee — fixed schedule, company tools, no other clients — FIRS or PenCom may reclassify the relationship. A reclassified contractor would trigger back-PAYE, pension arrears, penalties, and interest. Always review contractor relationships periodically and use formal contractor agreements to document the independent nature of the engagement.

## Minimum wage
The national minimum wage is ₦70,000 per month. This applies to all employees on the payroll. Workers earning below the minimum wage should be brought to compliance before the next payroll run.

## Pension employer threshold
- 15 or more workers: pension is mandatory for all qualifying employees.
- 3 to 15 workers: the law is silent, but remitting pension is strongly advisable and is the safer approach.
- Fewer than 3 workers: pension contributions are voluntary.

## Common questions

**Does pension apply to casual workers?**
Yes, but only once they have been engaged for 30 or more days. A casual worker engaged for fewer than 30 days is exempt from pension contributions entirely. The moment the engagement reaches or exceeds 30 days — even if it started as a short-term arrangement — pension becomes mandatory: 8% from the worker and a minimum of 10% from the employer, remitted within 7 days of salary payment.

**Do contractors get pension?**
No. Contractors are not employees and are not included in pension calculations. The only statutory deduction from a contractor payment is 5% Withholding Tax (WHT), which the paying company deducts and remits to FIRS by the 21st of the following month.

**What is the difference between a casual worker and a contractor?**
A casual worker is on the payroll — PAYE and NSITF apply, and pension applies if they work 30 or more days. A contractor is off the payroll entirely — no PAYE, no pension, no NSITF. Only WHT applies to contractor payments. Misclassifying a contractor who works exclusively for your business as a casual worker, or vice versa, carries penalty risk from FIRS and PenCom.
